# Learning from "Break the Pattern" AI Event

I attended **"Airess x Women Techmakers x GDG Mississauga: Break the Pattern"**, a community tech event focused on AI learning and collaboration.

The event was hosted by **Airess**, **GDG Mississauga**, and **Women Techmakers**, and brought together people interested in technology, AI, and building products.

### Event Schedule

6:00–6:05 pm | Arrival  
6:05–6:30 pm | Welcome & Intro Circles  
6:30–7:00 pm | Panel and Q&A  
7:00–7:40 pm | Build Challenge  
7:40–8:00 pm | Show & Tell

### Build Challenge

During the build challenge, participants worked in small teams to design an app using **Google AI Studio**.

The scenario was about **Sarah**, who wants to learn machine learning.  
She searched online and saved **38 bookmarks** and checked about **20 courses**, but she never actually started learning.

The challenge question was:

**How can we break this pattern and help Sarah take action?**

Each group discussed ideas and designed a solution to help users move from **collecting resources** to **actually starting learning**.

## Positive Aspects of the Event

The atmosphere was **friendly, welcoming, and collaborative**.  
There were group discussion sessions where participants could share ideas and connect with each other.

One thing I found particularly interesting was seeing **how different engineers and tech professionals approach building applications**.

For example:

- Some people focused on **defining the user persona first**
- Others prioritized **UI/UX and web design**
- Some started with **technical architecture**

Everyone had a different strategy for designing and building applications.

## A Structured Way to Design an App

One participant stood out because she was **extremely good at organizing conversations with complete strangers**.

I think she was effective because she clearly understood **which factors need to be aligned when designing an app**.

She structured the discussion like this:

### Understanding the User

- Who is the user?
- Background
- Motivation
- Willingness to pay

### Factors for Ranking Courses

- Tuition
- Time commitment
- Course length
- Homework requirements
- Technical background

### Product Design

- Goal at the end of the course
- Entry point
- Expected output

This framework made the discussion very productive and showed how structured thinking can help when designing products.

Possible answers included:

However, many people answered:

This made a strong impression on me.

## Reflection: My Weakness in Self-Introduction

During conversations, people often asked questions like:

- What's your background?
- What field are you trying to enter?
- What tech stack do you use?

These questions forced me to **summarize my skills and goals quickly**.

I realized that **my answers were weak and unclear**.

For example, my response sounded like this:

> I just graduated from college. I learned basic things...

This kind of answer does not attract much interest.

I also struggled to answer simple technical questions like:

> What stack do you use?

A clearer answer could be something like:

> I mainly work with React and Node.js, and I’m currently building small web applications to practice full-stack development.

## What I Learned

From this event, I learned several important lessons:

- Engineers approach problem-solving in **very different ways**
- **Structured thinking** is essential when designing products
- **Networking and community involvement** can create unexpected opportunities
- Being able to **clearly explain your technical skills** is extremely important

Technical ability alone is not enough.  
Being able to **communicate your work and ideas clearly** is also a key skill for developers.
